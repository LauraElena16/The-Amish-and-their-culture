The way of life

Family and personal life

                The Amish family is the basis of the Amish way of life. The family structure and traditions that seem to be taken from a page in history have remained an integral part of Amish culture. They have an unwritten plan for the Amish life called Ordnung, which guides them through all the details of daily life. To us, these rules might seem extremely legalistic. But the Amish consider the Ordnung as a sacred trust that separates them from the outside world. They bind them together in search of eternal salvation and create a desire for unity and conformity. When its members live together, work together, worship together and socialize together, the Amish family grows stronger.

                Amish men and women usually take on traditional gender roles. Spouses are primarily responsible for the financial well-being of the family. Wives usually dedicate themselves to housekeeping and motherhood. As in most families, gender roles in Amish marriages vary by personality. In non-farm families, the husband is usually the main supporter, but in cases where a wife owns a business, she can provide most of the family's income. When husbands work at home, there is often a considerable division of roles - women helping in the barn or shop and men helping in the garden or around the house.

           Women with young children almost never work full-time jobs outside the home, although some run home shops, greenhouses or bakeries. In some communities, single women or those whose children are raised run their own businesses.

            Amish women participate in household decision-making and children's discipline, even if they claim the man's role as the religious leader of the household. Although the father serves as the spiritual head of the house, the mothers are very active in feeding the children's spiritual life. The husband is responsible for religious matters related to the church and the outside world. Family members help each other in case of emergency, fire or flood and, of course, when death occurs.

              Friends don't have old people's homes. Elderly people normally live in an apartment in the house of one of their children or in a Grossdawdy Haus house. Appreciated for their wisdom, elders find meaning and dignity while caring for their adult children.

School and education

             An Amish education consists of eight years in a one-room school, taught by an eighth-grade teacher. In reality, this seems to be an extremely inappropriate and unfair way to prepare Amish children for adult life as productive members of so​ciety The purpose Amish education is not to promote individuality and critical thinking. The goal is teaching children the worth of hard work, ethical living, and how to be a valuable member of the Amish community. Amish education does not seek to create artists, scientists, musicians or actors. 
 

              The mission of Amish education is to teach the skills that are needed to lead a useful Amish life while developing the ability to function and do business in the outside world. But it also promotes separateness from the world. Amish view education as the responsibility of the parents, the school and the entire community acting, as always, under the influence the church.

For Amish people, children are looked as a gift from God. From birth until they start wal​king children are cared and caressed by all members of the family. 

               During the period between when a small child starts walking and when the child starts school, Amish education begins at home. Children speak only the Amish German dialect before they start school. They are taught to be obedient to older members of the family. They are taught to share with their siblings. They are assigned chores to create a good attitude toward work. Amish pre-school children are given a lot of freedom to tag along with adults and older siblings as they go through daily chores and routines observing and learning. The Amish have no pre-schools. They believe that is important that very young children are with their parents and learning under their guidance.

                At age six, Amish children start first grade. They go to a one-room school that includes grades one through eight. There are usually 30-35 scholars, many of which are siblings and cousins. School is usually very close to home. English, reading, writing, arithmetic and history are taught in Amish school. These are considered all the skills that are needed to lead an Amish life and yet be able to do business with the outside world.

               Following the old traditions of one-room schools, classes begin at 8:30am and end at 3:30pm. The day is divided into 4 periods with recess and lunch between. At recess and lunch, the children play and do a lot of exercise. They will be played in the playground that is found at each school. The day begins with bible reading, hymn singing, and reciting the prayer of God. Religion is not taught in school. This is the responsibility of the parents and the church.

Teachers are chosen from community members. A new teacher is usually a young girl. It is very important that she be strong in the faith and stick to the Amish way of thinking as she teaches the children. Of course, she must have the intelligence and ability to teach all school subjects.

The new teacher first serves an apprenticeship with an experienced teacher. When she is ready, she starts teaching at the school where she was hired. For help as he gains experience, he can attend meetings with other local teachers to discuss issues and exchange ideas.

               After eighth grade, Amish education moves from school, back home and community. Some boys return to the farm to continue learning agricultural skills. Others could be apprentices to Amish store owners or craftsmen who learn skills that will ensure their existence for themselves and their future families. The girls return home to work with their mothers, polishing their home skills.

Wedding tradition

                Amish weddings were traditionally held on Tuesdays or Thursdays, later in the autumn, after harvest. Now weddings are held throughout the year and on other days to accommodate the large number of couples getting married. A couple will not announce their engagement until just a few weeks before the wedding. They usually do not wear white, but one of the most acceptable colors in their group. They are very careful in choosing fabrics and colors.

                 Ohio Amish Country weddings are usually a time where English friends are invited to join, both at work and in the meal following. Preparation for the meal requires lots of help from the community. That's because the meal usually serves 200-600 persons. A group of women will gather a day or two before the wedding to set up the tables in the house.

                  The head table has places set for the wedding party and their attendants. This group could number up to 20. There are special napkins with the couple's names on them, pens with the date commemorated and other favors. Because of the size of the group, a cooking tent is often set up to accommodate the portable stoves and food preparation areas needed for such large groups. Amish weddings are held during a service similar to the typical Amish worship service. The Bishop and ministers preside. The couple takes their vows before the church.

                  Afterwards the celebration continues with the meal. The Amish wedding meal is usually typical fare with fried or roasted chicken, mashed potatoes and gravy and other vegetables. The typical foods at an Amish wedding have sparked a cottage industry in making Amish wedding foods. They can be found in some of the local markets.

                  After the meal, in some groups, the bride and groom visit the wedding meal guests with baskets of candy to hand out as favors. This gives them an opportunity to greet all the guests personally. After the gifts are opened and the guests begin to thin, it is time for the evening meal. The crowd is usually much smaller by now. In some groups, the parent's will give their newlyweds their first bedroom suite, handmade by the family or by another craftsman in the community.

                  The young couple will typically sleep in the parent's home the first night. In some groups the couple has to arise on the first morning of their married life and wash all their clothes together. They take the couple's bed out into a field along with a night stand on which they would place a lantern so they could find it.

                  Many times the couple will set up housekeeping with one of the parents until they can get on their feet and either buy, rent or build a new home. The wife may continue to work part time outside the home, continuing her cleaning business or working in a local establishment. But they soon retire to begin their family.

Clothing

                Amish are also called "Ordinary People" and one of the main reasons they are called because of the way they dress. You will never see models on any of their clothing and all the suits for men and the dresses for women are all plain in color. The rules require the use of only solid colors, and the colors usually consist of black, blue, burgundy, brown, purple or green.

Amish men

                 In all districts of the Amish community men wear black colored suits that are fastened with a hook and eye and have no outside pockets. The trousers that the men wear have no zippers and instead have buttons that button up the flap. One or two suspenders also hold up the trousers since no belts are allowed because of the flashy buckle.

                 For the church, Amish men wear a plain white shirt with a black coat and vest overtop. Some men also wear black bow ties. The suit coat has no buttons or collars because these are all reminiscent of military uniforms that represent the war that pacifist Amish reject. All of the men and boys also wear either straw hats, for the summertime, or black felt hats, for the wintertime or for formal occasions, every day.

                 Young men are clean shaven prior to marriage, while married men are required to let their beards grow. Mustaches are forbidden because they are considered to be adornment. Haircuts are typically block cut in the back and longer than most English styles.

                 The men typically wear broad brimmed felt hats in the winter. The width of the brim and the shape of the dome vary with the group. During the summer most groups will allow their men to wear stroh hoots or straw hats. The style of straw hat also lends a clue to the group the man is from. In the winter in some northern climates men are allowed to wear tsiple kops or knit caps.

Amish women

When it comes to Amish women’s wardrobes, their wardrobes are pretty small. They only believe they need four dresses.

               Amish women wear solid-color dresses with long sleeves and a full skirt, covered with a cape on the bodice. Some less conservative groups allow the women to wear short sleeved dresses but never sleeveless. Clothing is fastened with straight pins or snaps, stockings are black cotton and shoes are also black. Amish women are not permitted to wear patterned clothing or jewelry.

A married woman wears a black cape and apron to church, with a plain colored dress underneath, and a single woman wears a white cape and apron to church, with a plain colored dress underneath. For working around the family farm, women typically wear a gray apron. A woman’s cape and apron is fastened with straight pins at the waist, in the front of their dress, to attach the two together. 

             Women go barefoot around the farm, and house, and wear black shoes and stockings for church services. Women also always wear prayer caps, or head coverings, on their head, so that if they ever feel the need to pray, throughout the day, their heads are covered and sacred to the God.

Amish women never cut their hair, typically wearing it in a braid or bun on the back of the head concealed with a small white cap called a covering.  An Amish woman would never be seen outside her home without her covering. When going to town or church they typically wear a stiff black bonnet over their covering.

Plain clothing marks the Amish as a people apart. It emphasizes modesty, and Amish believe there is Biblical basis for wearing it. Plain clothing has strong symbolic and religious value for the Amish, and is one of the key visual markers of the culture. Clothing is a marker that not only identifies the Amish as different from the rest of the world, but also marks Amish communities as different from one another.

Furniture

             Amish furniture is furniture made by Amish people, mainly from Pennsylvania, Indiana and Ohio. This furniture is generally known to be handmade, made entirely of wood. The styles most often used by Amish wood​workers are generally traditional.

Great attention is paid to the details of the wood in the furniture manufacturing process. Each piece of wood is manually selected to match the specific furniture.

Amish furniture is made from a variety of hardwoods, including Nordic red oak, crushed white oak, cherry, maple, beech, elm, mahogany, walnut, hickory, cedar and pine. Nordic red oak is a very popular choice for American consumers for its warmth, color and durability.

Although the Amish may not drive or use electricity in their homes, they offer a wide range of furniture styles ranging from classic to contemporary. And, regardless of style, you can trust that everything is done with their attention without compromising on quality.

Music

                  The Amish hold worship services every two weeks in their homes or barns. Sunday morning singing is limited to hymns from the Ausbund, a collection of hymns written by early Anabaptists.

                  The songs are sung slowly, with many passing notes from one syllable to the next. It can take five minutes to sing a single verse. At funerals no hymns are sung, but a preacher usually reads a hymn. Written music is not used during worship. 

                   Besides their Ausbund hymns, many Amish also sing popular hymns in English at informal occasions, such as singings for the youth and family gatherings and visiting with neighbors. Simple instruments, while not used in worship nor formally approved, are found in social settings.
